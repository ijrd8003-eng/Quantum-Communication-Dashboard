Bell States

A Bell State is a specific quantum state of two qubits representing the simplest and most fundamental example of maximal quantum entanglement. In this lab, we build the state:

|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|00\rangle + |11\rangle)

Applying a Hadamard gate ($H$) on the first qubit puts it into superposition, and a Controlled-NOT ($CNOT$) gate with the first qubit as control and the second as target entangles them. Measurements on both qubits will yield correlated results ($00$ or $11$) with a 50% probability each.

Quantum Entanglement

Quantum Entanglement is a phenomenon where two or more particles become correlated in such a way that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the others.

\text{Entangling Qubits: } H(q_0) \rightarrow CNOT(q_0, q_1)

This creates a unified system. When you measure qubit 0, its wavefunction collapses, immediately forcing qubit 1 to collapse into the exact same corresponding state, regardless of the distance between them. This violates classical intuition and is the foundation for quantum communication.

Quantum Teleportation

Quantum Teleportation is a protocol to transmit a quantum state (an unknown qubit $|\psi\rangle$) from Alice to Bob. It uses classical bits and a pre-shared entangled pair (EPR pair).

\text{Teleport: } |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \quad (\text{Qubit } q_0 \rightarrow q_2)

Alice and Bob first share an entangled state. Alice performs a Bell measurement on the target qubit and her half of the entangled pair. She sends the two classical measurement outcomes to Bob, who performs corresponding corrections ($X$ or $Z$) to reconstruct the original state $|\psi\rangle$ on his qubit.
